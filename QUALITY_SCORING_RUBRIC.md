# QUALITY SCORING RUBRIC

**Version:** 1.0  
**Created:** 2026-05-23  
**Purpose:** Measure profile quality consistently across ascension phases

---

## Overview

The scoring rubric measures 8 dimensions of profile quality. Each dimension scores 0-10, with clear definitions for each level.

**Overall Profile Score** = Average of all section scores

---

## Dimension 1: SPECIFICITY

**Question:** Could this apply to 50 other people in the room?

### 0-2: Generic
- Applies to 80%+ of professionals
- Examples: "handles stress," "detail-oriented," "organized"
- Red flag: Same description fits multiple people

### 3-4: Recognizable Pattern
- Applies to 30-50% of similar roles
- Describes a common type
- Example: "task-focused, deadline-driven" (could be many people)

### 5-6: Moderately Specific
- Applies to 10-15% of professionals
- Clear pattern but could still describe others in similar role
- Example: "driven by system-building, frustrated by ad-hoc"

### 7-8: Specific
- Applies to 2-5% of people
- Distinctive constellation of behaviors
- Example: "prioritizes speed over consultation; accepts political cost; uncomfortable with ambiguity in operational clarity"

### 9-10: Highly Specific
- Applies to <1% of professionals
- Only makes sense for this particular person
- Includes distinctive behavior + reasoning + cost
- Example: "builds operational infrastructure with minimal supervision but burns out managing chaos; chooses to stay in roles where firefighting is normalized"

---

## Dimension 2: CAUSAL DEPTH

**Question:** Is the "why" explained, or just the "what"?

### 0-2: Description Only
- States what happens
- No explanation of mechanism
- Example: "Jordan is decisive"

### 3-4: Simple Causation
- One-level explanation
- Example: "Jordan is decisive because she values speed"
- Missing: Why does she value speed? What is she protecting?

### 5-6: Mechanism + Consequence
- Explains how it works
- Shows one outcome
- Example: "Prioritizes speed because ambiguity creates anxiety; this works in startup but creates blind spots in mature organizations"
- Missing: Why the anxiety? What identity is at stake?

### 7-8: Mechanism + Multiple Consequences
- Multiple cause-effect chains
- Explains what accumulates
- Example: "Prioritizes speed because ambiguity creates anxiety (protecting image of competence); works in chaos but creates dependency on her judgment; blocks others from developing crisis management skills"

### 9-10: Full Causal Chain
- Root cause → behavior → immediate consequence → organizational consequence → long-term cost
- Explains identity stakes and what would need to change
- Includes what-breaks-under-pressure

---

## Dimension 3: EVIDENCE GROUNDING

**Question:** Is this observed or speculated?

### 0-2: Speculative
- Interpretation without behavioral support
- Assumes psychological states
- Example: "suffers from imposter syndrome"
- Missing: What specific behavior indicates this?

### 3-4: Weak Evidence
- Plausible interpretation
- Single behavior pattern
- Could be explained other ways
- Example: "Avoids public speaking (assumes fear)"
- Missing: Verification of the assumption

### 5-6: Moderately Grounded
- Multiple consistent behaviors point in same direction
- Inferred but consistent
- Example: "Avoids public speaking, rarely volunteers opinions in large meetings, becomes animated in one-on-ones"
- Missing: Clarity on confidence level

### 7-8: Well Grounded
- Multiple direct evidence or clear behavioral pattern
- Distinction between high confidence + medium confidence elements
- Example: "Avoids public speaking [90% confidence from observation], likely due to audience anxiety rather than lack of confidence [60% confidence from context]"

### 9-10: Solidly Grounded
- Direct evidence + pattern + pressure-behavior observation
- Clear confidence calibration
- Explicitly marks uncertainties
- Example: "Avoids speaking in group >5 people [direct observation], performs well in small group [direct], becomes quite animated with peers [direct], likely mechanism is anxiety about perceived judgment [moderate confidence from pattern]"

---

## Dimension 4: ORGANIZATIONAL USEFULNESS

**Question:** Would this change how someone manages/works with this person?

### 0-2: Irrelevant
- Interesting but disconnected from work
- Example: Personality type, childhood patterns
- Manager response: "Nice to know, doesn't change how I work with them"

### 3-4: Context
- Adds background understanding
- Doesn't change behavior
- Example: "Values achievement because of competitive childhood"
- Manager response: "Explains motivation, but doesn't change management"

### 5-6: Moderately Useful
- Suggests what might work/not work
- Example: "Responds to clarity and autonomy"
- Manager response: "Good to know, will try that approach"

### 7-8: Directly Actionable
- Clear implications for working with/managing this person
- Example: "Will escalate chaos internally rather than admit need for resources; manager should proactively check overcommitment or risk burnout + sudden departure"
- Manager response: "Changes my management approach immediately"

### 9-10: Transforms Understanding
- Fundamentally changes how relationship works
- Identifies hidden dynamics
- Example: "Appears decisive but avoids the organizational conflict required to implement decisions; will execute someone else's decision faster than pushing her own; structure your delegation around this"
- Manager response: "This completely reframes how I work with them"

---

## Dimension 5: EMOTIONAL REALISM

**Question:** Does this ring true in lived experience?

### 0-2: Clinical/Sterile
- Sounds like assessment language
- Disconnected from human experience
- Example: "Exhibits traits of conscientiousness with undertones of perfectionism"

### 3-4: Accurate but Impersonal
- True but doesn't capture felt experience
- Example: "Works hard and cares about results"

### 5-6: Recognizable
- Captures real pattern
- Some authentic language
- Example: "Puts in long hours when work feels important; gets frustrated when others don't match that investment"

### 7-8: Emotionally Resonant
- Captures not just what happens but how it feels
- Language that rings true
- Example: "Works until the problem is solved, gets quietly frustrated when others clock out; doesn't blame them but internally questions whether they care as much"

### 9-10: Deeply True
- Captures the interior experience
- Language that person recognizes themselves in
- Example: "Solves problems faster than discussing them, which works until the problem is someone else's incompetence; then becomes quietly furious while maintaining professional demeanor; costs: isolation, resentment, burnout"

---

## Dimension 6: GENERIC LANGUAGE LEAKAGE

**Question:** Does it use banned phrases or consultant clichés?

### 0-2: Heavy Clichés
- Multiple banned phrases
- Sounds like boilerplate assessment
- Example: "Natural leader with growth potential, emotional intelligence, and ability to inspire teams"

### 3-4: Some Clichés
- 5-10 consultant phrases
- Mixed generic + specific
- Example: "Strong executor with clear strengths, though room to grow in stakeholder communication"

### 5-6: Mostly Specific
- 1-3 generic phrases
- Mostly behavioral language
- Example: "Drives execution but sometimes assumes others understand without explicit communication"

### 7-8: Very Specific
- Zero banned phrases
- Some generic words but used specifically
- Example: "Focuses on execution over explanation; works with detail-oriented peers; struggles with cross-functional partners who need more context"

### 9-10: Zero Clichés
- All language is specific and behavioral
- Every phrase could not be replaced with something generic
- Uses concrete examples, not abstract traits

---

## Dimension 7: CONTRADICTION DEPTH

**Question:** Are contradictions explained or just named?

### 0-2: No Contradictions
- Presents unified narrative
- No tension identified
- Missing: Real people are contradictory

### 3-4: Contradictions Named
- Identifies "on one hand... on the other"
- Doesn't explain why
- Example: "Appears rational but gets emotional about inefficiency"
- Missing: Why does this happen? What would resolve it?

### 5-6: Contradictions With Surface Explanation
- Names tension + suggests why
- Missing: What identity is protected? What breaks under pressure?
- Example: "Positions as objective but is frustrated by inefficiency; masks frustration as 'just pointing out facts'"

### 7-8: Contradictions Explained
- Why tension exists + what identity is at stake
- Shows cost of maintaining contradiction
- Example: "Frames self as systems-thinker (rational, objective) but gets internally furious at inefficiency (emotional, subjective); maintains this by framing emotions as 'logical frustration'; costs: isolation from others, difficulty asking for help because it contradicts image of competence"

### 9-10: Contradictions Fully Analyzed
- Complete analysis: tension, identity stake, behavior that maintains it, cost, pressure-point where contradiction breaks
- Example: "Positions as systems-focused (rational, analytical) but avoids the interpersonal conflict required to fix known systems (emotional avoidance). Maintains this through 'I'm waiting for organizational buy-in' story. Costs: problems persist, others absorb hidden work. Breaking point: crisis where delay costs more than the conflict would have."

---

## Dimension 8: HALLUCINATION RISK

**Question:** Is this observation or invention?

### 0-2: High Risk
- Significant speculation
- Psychological interpretation without support
- Example: "Childhood trauma explains defensive behavior"
- Risk: Completely wrong and harmful

### 3-4: Moderate-High Risk
- Some grounding but significant inference
- Example: "Perfectionism suggests fear of judgment"
- Risk: Plausible but not verified

### 5-6: Moderate Risk
- Mostly grounded + some inference
- Distinguishes observation from interpretation
- Example: "Perfectionist behaviors (observation); likely protecting against criticism (inference with moderate confidence)"

### 7-8: Low Risk
- Clearly distinguishes grounded + speculative
- Marks confidence levels
- Example: "[90% confident from observation] Perfectionist behaviors; [60% confident] likely protecting against criticism; [uncertain] whether this is personality or learned from environment"

### 9-10: Very Low Risk
- Only states what's directly observed
- Explicitly marks all inference as speculation
- Avoids psychological interpretation
- Example: "Re-does work 2-3x before submission [observed]; spends 2x time on her tasks vs peers [observed]; becomes irritable when work is interrupted [observed]; mechanism unknown [explicit uncertainty]"

## SCORING EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Weak Profile

```
Identity Narrative:
"Jordan is a strong leader who cares about people and drives results.
She's organized, detail-oriented, and committed to excellence."

Scoring:
- Specificity: 1/10 (applies to 80% of professionals)
- Causal Depth: 1/10 (only "what," no "why")
- Evidence Grounding: 2/10 (generic, unsupported)
- Organizational Usefulness: 2/10 (doesn't change management)
- Emotional Realism: 2/10 (clinical language)
- Generic Language Leakage: 2/10 (full of clichés)
- Contradiction Depth: 0/10 (none)
- Hallucination Risk: 6/10 (moderate)

AVERAGE: 1.75/10 (WEAK)
```

### Example 2: Medium Profile

```
Strategic Ceiling:
"Jordan builds operational systems efficiently but struggles to adapt them
when organizational scale changes. She excels at 10-20 person teams but hits
a wall at 50+ because her approach requires her personal involvement."

Scoring:
- Specificity: 7/10 (distinctive but plausible for ops leaders)
- Causal Depth: 6/10 (names consequence but not root cause)
- Evidence Grounding: 7/10 (behavioral pattern + scaling logic)
- Organizational Usefulness: 7/10 (changes hiring/structure decisions)
- Emotional Realism: 6/10 (accurate but slightly abstract)
- Generic Language Leakage: 7/10 (one phrase: "builds...efficiently")
- Contradiction Depth: 5/10 (hints at contradiction, not explained)
- Hallucination Risk: 3/10 (low speculative content)

AVERAGE: 6.25/10 (GOOD, needs upgrade)
```

### Example 3: Strong Profile

```
Contradiction Analysis:
"Jordan frames herself as systems-focused and objective, but avoids the
interpersonal conflict required to actually implement systems changes.
She'll analyze a problem thoroughly, propose a solution, then decline to
push it if there's organizational resistance. She resolves this contradiction
by reframing non-action as 'waiting for organizational readiness' rather than
acknowledging she's choosing comfort over impact.

Cost: Problems persist, hidden work falls on others. She becomes quietly
frustrated because her advice isn't taken, but won't take the risks required
for real influence. Breaking point: When crisis forces a choice between
admitting the conflict or absorbing the fallout herself—then she acts, but
too late."

Scoring:
- Specificity: 8/10 (only applies to people who combine analysis + conflict-avoidance)
- Causal Depth: 9/10 (full chain: identity stake → behavior → cost → breaking point)
- Evidence Grounding: 8/10 (pattern-based + behavioral observation)
- Organizational Usefulness: 9/10 (completely changes how to work with her)
- Emotional Realism: 8/10 (rings true in lived experience)
- Generic Language Leakage: 9/10 (zero clichés)
- Contradiction Depth: 10/10 (fully analyzed)
- Hallucination Risk: 2/10 (very low, grounded in pattern)

AVERAGE: 8.125/10 (STRONG, approaching "holy shit")
```

## Quality Gates

### MUST REACH: 8.5/10 or higher
- Before deploying new inference logic
- Across all new profiles generated post-ascension
- Verified by independent review

### NICE TO HAVE: 9.0/10 or higher
- For profiles used in marketing/case studies
- For profiles of major clients
- Indicates "holy shit" threshold likely reached

### DEFECT: Below 6.0/10
- Triggers manual review
- Cannot be deployed
- Requires rewrite before release
