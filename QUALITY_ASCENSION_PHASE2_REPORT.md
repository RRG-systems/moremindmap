- Retention risk: High performers leave faster than they can be replaced

**What this requires Jordan to surrender:**
- Identity as irreplaceable fixer
- Control over crisis management
- Certainty that comes from direct involvement
- Ability to avoid delegation/relationship building

### 2. BEHAVIORAL CONSEQUENCES (NEW SECTION)

**Note:** Baseline did not have explicit behavioral consequences analysis.

#### AFTER (Phase 2 Creation)

```
Jordan's pattern creates three downstream effects that compound over time:

1. Organizational learned helplessness
Because Jordan catches dropped balls, the organization never learns capacity 
discipline. The CEO continues overcommitting (board pressure gets absorbed). 
The team waits to see if Jordan will catch problems rather than escalating 
early. When crises hit, everyone looks to Jordan—not because he's uniquely 
capable, but because he's the only one who's practiced. The capability gap 
isn't talent—it's that Jordan's intervention prevents others from developing 
the skill.

2. Invisible coordination debt
Jordan avoids naming broken structures until crisis forces it. This means 
problems persist longer than necessary. Each time he absorbs a crisis without 
addressing the root cause, technical debt accumulates. Rushed fixes instead 
of systemic improvement. The organization compounds workarounds instead of 
building stable foundations. What feels like efficiency ("Jordan fixes it 
fast") is actually efficiency theater—the same problems recur because the 
structural issues remain.

3. Relationship withdrawal pattern
Jordan protects energy by positioning as "cold/rational." Others interpret 
this as disengagement or arrogance. Relational distance increases. Fewer 
people bring problems early (they wait until it's undeniable). This means 
Jordan only sees problems in crisis state, which reinforces his belief that 
"the organization isn't ready" for proactive systems work. It's a self-
fulfilling loop: distance → late visibility → crisis intervention → 
validation of firefighting mode → continued distance.

What this costs the organization:
- Talent degradation: High performers leave rather than repeatedly compensate
- Coordination brittleness: Knowledge concentrates in Jordan (single point of failure)
- Decision latency: Unclear when to escalate vs wait for Jordan to catch it
- Cultural debt: Crisis absorption becomes normalized operating model
- Strategic drift: Firefighting crowds out actual systems building

What this costs Jordan:
- Leverage atrophy: His actual strength (process design) doesn't get used
- Reputation narrowing: Known as fixer, not systems architect
- Energy depletion: Sustaining crisis mode is unsustainable (his word)
- Identity trap: Can't prove he's capable of building sustainable systems because he's trapped fixing broken ones
```
